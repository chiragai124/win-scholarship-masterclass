Scholarship Myths — Busted
Myth

You need perfect grades to get a scholarship

Many students believe only toppers can apply. This stops a huge number of deserving people.

Truth

A strong profile matters far more than grades

Rajratna himself received a 70% scholarship despite not being a "bright student always."

Myth

International students don't get funding

A widespread belief that keeps international applicants from even trying.

Truth

Many scholarships are specifically for international students

Your story, impact potential, and profile are what committees are really looking for.

🎓
Types of Scholarships

Fully Funded

Covers tuition, living costs, and travel. You pay nothing to the university or the country. Everything is taken care of.

Partially Funded

Covers a portion — typically 50–70% of tuition or living costs. Rajratna received this type (Vice Chancellor Scholarship, Birmingham).

Sports & Athletic

For students looking to make a meaningful contribution to their sport or revolutionize their athletic discipline.

Merit-Based

Good grades matter, but committees also want leadership, volunteering, and community impact alongside academic records.

🔍
What Scholarship Committees Actually Look For
🌍

Impact Potential

Not just what you've done — but what the committee believes you will go on to do with their investment. Are you solving a real problem in your community or country?

🤝

Leadership

Have you led people? Started an initiative? Brought change to your community? Scholarship bodies invest in those who give back — not just those who take forward.

🎯

Mission Alignment

Your reason for applying must match what the scholarship stands for. Sports scholarships want athleticism. Leadership scholarships want proven initiative. Align your story to the scholarship's purpose.

✍️
SOP Formula That Actually Works

Your Statement of Purpose is not a resume in paragraph form. It's a story that shows the committee why the world needs you to study this — and why they should fund it.

1

The Hook — A Real Problem

Open with a vivid, real moment that made you realise there's a problem worth solving. Pull the reader in from the first sentence.

2

Why This Problem? Why This Program?

Connect the problem you saw to the degree you're pursuing. Explain why this specific university or program is the right place to solve it.

3

Future Vision — Short & Long Term

Be specific. After the degree, what will you do in Year 1? Year 5? What initiative, project, or change will you drive? Numbers and details make this believable.

4

Strong Closing — Circle Back

End where you began. Bring the story full circle. The reader should feel the journey is complete and that you — specifically — must be funded.

The water meter outside my grandmother's house in Nagpur had not moved in three days, yet her tap kept running. That contradiction sent me, at 16, into the municipal records office — and into the realisation that most water loss problems are not engineering failures, but data failures. Over the next four years, this question became my undergraduate thesis on low-cost IoT leak sensors, then a pilot across 40 households...

— Sample SOP opening shared during the session

Common Questions from the Session
Q1

Is the Amber Scholarship fully funded?

▾
No. Amber Scholarship is not fully funded. It is a $50,000 pool distributed among selected students. The money is yours to use however you need — for academics, living, or your goals.
Q2

Is it first-come-first-served?

▾
No. All applications received before 31st August 2026 will be evaluated on the strength of the profile, passion, and alignment with the scholarship category. Submitting early gives no advantage.
Q3

How many scholars will be selected?

▾
The presenter will confirm this with the scholarship team and share the update in the Amber community group. Keep an eye on the group for announcements.
Q4

I already applied once. Should I apply again?

▾
No need to reapply if your first submission was complete and strong. One well-crafted application is enough. The team will evaluate what you submitted.
Q5

I'm struggling to write my SOP. What should I do?

▾
Use the 4-step formula covered in this session: Start with a real hook (a problem you witnessed), explain why this program helps solve it, share specific short- and long-term goals, and close by bringing the story full circle. The SOP section above covers this in detail.
Q6

After Class 12, what are the steps to study abroad?

▾
Step 1: Choose a course you are passionate about. Step 2: Shortlist universities that offer that course. Step 3: Check each university's entrance exam requirements. Step 4: Appear for required tests (IELTS, etc.). Step 5: Start building your profile. Then think about scholarships once you have an acceptance letter.
Q7

Is the scholarship available for students from Pakistan?

▾
A participant raised a concern about not seeing Pakistan as an option during signup. The presenter asked them to post the issue in the community group and committed to finding a workaround so that Pakistani students can apply.
